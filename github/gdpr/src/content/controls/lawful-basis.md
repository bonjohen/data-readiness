Article 6 of the GDPR requires that every processing activity rests on one of six lawful bases: consent, contract performance, legal obligation, vital interests, public task, or legitimate interests. Selecting the correct basis is not a formality — it determines which data subject rights apply, shapes transparency obligations, and affects whether the processing can continue if circumstances change. Organizations that default to consent when another basis is more appropriate often create unnecessary compliance burdens and fragile legal footing.

Legitimate interest assessments deserve particular attention because they require a three-part balancing test: identifying the legitimate interest, demonstrating that processing is necessary to achieve it, and balancing it against the data subject's rights and freedoms. The assessment must be documented before processing begins and revisited when the nature of the processing or the context changes. Supervisory authorities frequently scrutinize LIAs, making thorough documentation essential.

Special category data under Art. 9 — including health data, biometric data, racial or ethnic origin, and political opinions — demands an additional layer of justification beyond the Art. 6 lawful basis. Organizations must identify a specific Art. 9(2) condition (such as explicit consent or substantial public interest) and document why that condition applies. Failure to maintain this dual-layer justification is one of the most common findings in regulatory audits.
