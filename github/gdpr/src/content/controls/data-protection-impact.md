Article 35 requires a Data Protection Impact Assessment whenever processing is likely to result in a high risk to the rights and freedoms of individuals. The regulation identifies three specific scenarios that always require a DPIA: systematic and extensive profiling with significant effects, large-scale processing of special category data, and systematic monitoring of publicly accessible areas. Supervisory authorities publish additional lists of processing types requiring DPIAs, and organizations should consult their relevant authority's list as a starting point.

A DPIA is not merely a risk register. It must include a systematic description of the processing and its purposes, an assessment of the necessity and proportionality of the processing relative to those purposes, an assessment of risks to data subjects, and the measures envisaged to address those risks. The DPO must be consulted during the DPIA process (Art. 35(2)), and the views of data subjects or their representatives should be sought where appropriate. When the DPIA indicates that residual risk remains high despite mitigation measures, Art. 36 requires prior consultation with the supervisory authority before processing begins.

Organizations should establish a screening process that evaluates every new processing activity against DPIA triggers, ensuring that high-risk processing is identified early in the project lifecycle rather than retroactively. DPIAs are not one-time documents — they must be reviewed and updated when the nature, scope, context, or purposes of processing change. This ongoing review is particularly important for iterative technology deployments where the risk profile evolves over time.
