Article 37 mandates DPO appointment for public authorities, organizations whose core activities involve regular and systematic monitoring of data subjects on a large scale, and organizations whose core activities involve large-scale processing of special category data or criminal conviction data. Even when not legally required, many organizations appoint a DPO voluntarily as a governance best practice. Whether mandatory or voluntary, once appointed, the DPO role carries the full set of protections and requirements specified in Articles 38 and 39.

The DPO's independence is a cornerstone of the role. Article 38(3) prohibits the organization from instructing the DPO on how to perform their tasks, and the DPO cannot be dismissed or penalized for performing their duties. The DPO must not hold a position that creates a conflict of interest — roles such as head of IT, head of HR, or general counsel are typically considered incompatible because they involve decisions about personal data processing that the DPO would need to oversee. The DPO must report directly to the highest management level and be given sufficient resources, access to processing operations, and opportunities to maintain expert knowledge.

Article 39 outlines the DPO's minimum tasks: informing and advising the organization on GDPR obligations, monitoring compliance, providing advice on DPIAs, cooperating with the supervisory authority, and acting as the contact point for the supervisory authority. The DPO's contact details must be published and communicated to the supervisory authority. Organizations should document how the DPO is involved in data protection matters — from new project consultations to breach response — as this involvement is a key indicator of accountability under Art. 5(2).
