Article 5 of DORA places explicit accountability for digital operational resilience at the highest level of the organization. The management body — board of directors, executive committee, or equivalent governing organ — must define, approve, and actively oversee the ICT risk management framework. This is not a delegable responsibility: while day-to-day execution may be performed by dedicated functions, the management body retains ultimate accountability for ensuring that ICT risks are managed proportionately and effectively.

This accountability extends to resource allocation. The management body must ensure that adequate budget and human resources are dedicated to digital operational resilience, covering ICT security, resilience testing, third-party risk management, and incident response capabilities. Underfunding digital resilience is effectively a governance failure under DORA, and competent authorities may assess whether resource allocation is commensurate with the entity's risk profile.

Mandatory training for management body members on ICT risks and digital operational resilience is a distinctive DORA requirement. Members must develop and maintain sufficient understanding of ICT risk to exercise meaningful oversight — not merely sign off on reports they cannot evaluate. This training should cover the threat landscape, the entity's ICT risk profile, regulatory requirements, and the operational resilience implications of strategic decisions such as technology platform choices, outsourcing arrangements, and digital transformation initiatives.
