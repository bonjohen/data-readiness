Several U.S. states impose registration requirements on data brokers — businesses that knowingly collect and sell or license the personal information of consumers with whom they do not have a direct relationship. Vermont was the first state to enact a data broker registration requirement (9 V.S.A. s 2446, effective January 2019), followed by California (Cal. Civ. Code s 1798.99.80, the Delete Act), Texas (TDPSA data broker provisions), and Oregon (OCPA data broker provisions). The definitions of "data broker" vary across states, and an organization may qualify as a data broker in one state but not another based on how each state defines "direct relationship" and what activities constitute "sale" or "licensing" of personal data.

| State | Registration Required | Annual Fee | Annual Report | Key Definition Elements |
|-------|----------------------|------------|---------------|------------------------|
| Vermont | Yes | $100 | Yes (by Jan 31) | Collects and sells data of consumers with no direct relationship |
| California | Yes (Delete Act) | $46,150+ (tiered) | Yes | Collects and sells/licenses data of consumers with no direct relationship |
| Texas | Yes | Varies | Yes | Data broker defined under TDPSA with additional deletion obligations |
| Oregon | Yes | Varies | Yes | Data broker provisions under OCPA |

The California Delete Act (SB 362) significantly expanded data broker obligations beyond mere registration. Data brokers registered in California must provide a mechanism for consumers to request deletion of their personal information, and the state is building a centralized deletion mechanism that will allow consumers to submit a single request to all registered data brokers. Organizations that qualify as data brokers in California face both registration and operational deletion infrastructure requirements, with penalties for non-registration of $200 per day.

Organizations should conduct a data broker status assessment for each state that has enacted registration requirements, analyzing whether their business activities fall within the state's definition. This assessment should be documented and revisited when business models change — for example, an organization that begins selling data analytics products derived from consumer data may newly qualify as a data broker. The registration and renewal calendar should be centrally managed, with automated reminders set at least 60 days before renewal deadlines. Annual reports should be prepared and filed on time, with copies retained as compliance evidence. For organizations that operate in the data ecosystem but do not believe they qualify as data brokers, the assessment documenting that conclusion is itself an important compliance artifact for any future AG inquiry.
