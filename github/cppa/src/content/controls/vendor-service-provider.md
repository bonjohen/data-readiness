The CCPA/CPRA draws sharp distinctions between service providers, contractors, and third parties, and each classification carries different contractual obligations. A service provider (§1798.140(ag)) processes PI on behalf of the business pursuant to a written contract that prohibits the service provider from retaining, using, or disclosing the PI for any purpose other than performing the services specified in the contract. Contractors (§1798.140(j)) are subject to similar restrictions but also must certify that they understand the limitations and will comply. Third parties (§1798.140(ai)) are entities that receive PI not under a service provider or contractor agreement, and disclosures to third parties may constitute a "sale" or "sharing" under the statute.

Written agreements must contain specific clauses: a prohibition on selling or sharing the PI received, a prohibition on retaining or using PI outside the direct business relationship, a requirement to comply with the CCPA/CPRA, a grant of the right to audit or test the service provider's compliance, and a requirement that the service provider notify the business if it can no longer meet its obligations. These are not optional best practices — they are statutory requirements that the business must be able to demonstrate in an enforcement action or audit.

The most common gap in vendor management is the failure to apply the same contractual and oversight discipline to subcontractors. When a service provider engages a subcontractor that will handle the business's PI, the same restrictions must flow down. Organizations should maintain a living vendor register that tracks each vendor's classification (service provider, contractor, or third party), the PI categories shared, the contractual basis, and the date of the last due diligence review. Periodic re-assessment is essential because vendor practices and the regulatory landscape both evolve.
