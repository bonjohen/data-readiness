COPPA imposes two distinct notice obligations: an online notice (the public-facing privacy policy) and a direct notice sent individually to parents before PI collection begins. The online notice under 16 CFR § 312.4(b) must identify the operator (name, physical address, email, phone), list the categories of personal information collected from children, explain how each category is used, describe whether PI is disclosed to third parties and for what purposes, and inform parents of their rights to review, delete, and refuse further collection. The notice must be written in clear, understandable language appropriate for a general audience, not buried in legal boilerplate.

The direct notice requirement at § 312.4(c) is separate and more targeted. Before collecting PI from a child, the operator must send the parent a notice describing the specific information it intends to collect, how it will be used, and the parent's options. This direct notice must also include a link to the online notice and provide a mechanism for the parent to respond. The FTC has emphasized that the direct notice is not satisfied by simply posting the privacy policy; it must be actively delivered to the parent through email or another direct channel.

Common compliance failures include privacy policies that use generic language without COPPA-specific disclosures, policies that list broad categories of data without specifying which apply to children, missing or broken links to the privacy policy on collection pages, and direct notices that are sent after collection has already begun rather than before. Organizations should audit their sites quarterly to verify link placement and conduct annual reviews of notice content against current collection practices.
