The CCPA/CPRA imposes layered notice obligations. At the point of collection, businesses must inform consumers about the categories of personal information being collected and the purposes for which those categories will be used (§1798.100(b)). Separately, the comprehensive privacy policy must list all categories collected in the preceding 12 months, the sources, the business or commercial purposes, the categories of third parties to whom PI is disclosed, and specific descriptions of consumer rights including the right to know, delete, correct, and opt out.

CPRA expanded the notice requirements to cover sensitive personal information, the right to limit its use, and new details about cross-context behavioral advertising. The privacy policy must also disclose whether the business sells or shares PI and, if so, the categories involved. The "Do Not Sell or Share" link must be a clearly labeled, standalone link on the homepage, not buried in a general privacy preferences menu.

Regulators and plaintiffs frequently target notice deficiencies because they are easy to identify and hard to defend. Common failures include: privacy policies that list PI categories generically rather than mapping them to specific purposes, at-collection notices that omit one or more intake channels (mobile apps, call centers, in-store kiosks), and "Do Not Sell" links that require consumers to navigate through multiple pages. Annual reviews should compare current data practices against the policy text to catch drift. Any new PI category, purpose, or vendor relationship should trigger an out-of-cycle review.
