Item 106(b) of Regulation S-K requires registrants to describe their cybersecurity risk management processes in sufficient detail for investors to evaluate the company's approach. This is not a checkbox exercise — the SEC expects a narrative description of how the company actually identifies, assesses, and manages cyber risks, what frameworks it uses (NIST CSF, ISO 27001, etc.), and how these processes connect to the broader enterprise risk management program. Companies that treat cybersecurity as a siloed IT function will find this disclosure difficult to write convincingly.

The integration with enterprise risk management is a key disclosure element. The SEC wants to understand whether cybersecurity risk is evaluated alongside financial, operational, legal, and strategic risks, or whether it exists in a parallel universe. Companies should describe the mechanisms that connect cyber risk assessment to business decision-making — for example, how cyber risk factors into M&A due diligence, product launch decisions, or vendor selection. The use of external assessors, consultants, and auditors should also be disclosed, as it signals the maturity and independence of the risk management program.

The forward-looking disclosure requirement — whether cyber risks have materially affected or are reasonably likely to materially affect the company — requires coordination between the CISO, CFO, and legal counsel. This is where the risk register becomes critical. Cyber risks should be rated using the same materiality framework used for other business risks, and the disclosure should reflect an honest assessment of the company's risk posture without inadvertently creating a roadmap for attackers.
