The SEC cybersecurity disclosure rules fundamentally changed incident response requirements for public companies. Traditional IR programs focused on containment, eradication, and recovery — the SEC rules add a parallel disclosure workstream that must operate on a compressed timeline. Your IR program must be able to detect an incident, investigate it sufficiently to support a materiality determination, and — if material — produce an 8-K filing within four business days of that determination. This requires tight integration between the security team, legal counsel, and investor relations from the earliest stages of incident response.

The IR plan should define clear roles and handoffs. The CISO owns technical investigation and initial impact assessment. Legal counsel drives the materiality analysis using the predefined framework. The board liaison ensures the appropriate committee is briefed according to the escalation matrix. Investor relations prepares disclosure language once materiality is determined. These workstreams must run in parallel, not sequentially — waiting for the technical investigation to complete before engaging legal and IR teams will blow the timeline.

Tabletop exercises are essential for testing this coordination. At least one annual exercise should simulate an SEC disclosure scenario end-to-end: incident detection, investigation, materiality determination, 8-K drafting, board notification, and investor communications. The exercise should test the timeline realistically — can your team actually move from materiality determination to filing in four business days? Document the exercise, its findings, and any process improvements, as these records serve as evidence of program maturity for both SEC inquiries and 10-K disclosure.
