Item 106(c)(2) requires registrants to describe management's role in assessing and managing material cybersecurity risks, including the relevant expertise of such management. This goes beyond simply naming the CISO — the SEC expects disclosure of what makes your security leadership qualified to manage the risks described elsewhere in the filing. Investors use this information to evaluate whether the company has the human capital to execute its stated cybersecurity risk management strategy.

The disclosure should cover the CISO's (or equivalent's) professional background, relevant certifications (CISSP, CISM, CRISC, etc.), years of experience, and reporting structure. The reporting line matters — a CISO who reports to the CIO is positioned differently than one who reports to the CEO or the board directly, and investors draw inferences from these structures. If other members of management have cybersecurity responsibilities (CTO, CRO, General Counsel), their relevant expertise should also be documented.

Ongoing professional development demonstrates that your security leadership stays current with evolving threats and regulations. Maintain records of conference attendance, continuing education credits, certifications renewed, and specialized training completed. This is also an area where gaps should be acknowledged internally — if your organization lacks certain expertise, the risk management disclosure should address how that gap is mitigated, whether through external advisors, managed security services, or recruitment plans.
