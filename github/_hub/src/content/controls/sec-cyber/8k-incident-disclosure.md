Form 8-K Item 1.05 requires registrants to disclose material cybersecurity incidents within four business days of determining that the incident is material. The clock starts at the materiality determination, not at incident discovery — a critical distinction that gives organizations time to investigate before the filing obligation triggers. However, companies cannot unreasonably delay the materiality determination itself. This control ensures your organization has the templates, workflows, and review processes to file accurately within the compressed timeline.

The disclosure must describe the nature, scope, and timing of the incident and its material impact or reasonably likely material impact on the registrant, including financial condition and results of operations. The SEC deliberately excluded a requirement to disclose specific technical details (attack vectors, vulnerabilities exploited) to avoid giving adversaries a roadmap. Additionally, the Attorney General may grant a delay if disclosure would pose a substantial risk to national security or public safety. Your filing process should include a decision tree for evaluating whether the exception applies and the procedure for requesting a delay.

For incidents that unfold over time, the SEC expects companies to file amendments (Form 8-K/A) as additional material information becomes available. Your process should define triggers for amendments, assign responsibility for monitoring ongoing incidents post-filing, and maintain a log connecting initial filings to subsequent amendments. Investor relations, legal, and the security team must coordinate closely throughout.
