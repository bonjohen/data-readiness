Section 314.4(f) requires financial institutions to take reasonable steps to select and retain service providers that are capable of maintaining appropriate safeguards for customer information. This obligation begins at vendor selection — before engaging a service provider that will access, store, or process customer information, the institution must evaluate the provider's security posture through questionnaires, SOC 2 reports, penetration test results, or other evidence of their security program maturity. A provider that cannot demonstrate adequate safeguards should not be entrusted with customer data regardless of their functional capabilities or pricing.

Contractual provisions are the enforcement mechanism for vendor security expectations. Every contract with a service provider handling customer information must include specific security requirements: implementation of appropriate safeguards, incident notification obligations with defined timelines, audit and assessment rights, data handling restrictions, and provisions for secure return or destruction of data upon contract termination. Generic language about "reasonable security" is insufficient — the contractual requirements should be specific enough to be measurable and enforceable.

Vendor oversight does not end at contract signing. The institution must periodically reassess whether service providers continue to meet their security obligations. This includes reviewing updated SOC reports, requesting evidence of ongoing security practices, monitoring for security incidents involving the provider, and conducting on-site assessments where warranted by the risk level. When a provider's security posture deteriorates or a material breach occurs, the institution must have a process for escalation, remediation, and if necessary, provider replacement. The vendor oversight program should be integrated with the risk assessment so that third-party risks are evaluated alongside internal risks.
