Article 30 of the GDPR requires organizations to maintain written records of their processing activities. For controllers, the ROPA must include the name and contact details of the controller and DPO, the purposes of processing, descriptions of data subject categories and personal data categories, recipient categories, international transfer details (including safeguards), envisaged retention periods, and a general description of technical and organizational security measures. Processors have a parallel obligation under Art. 30(2) covering the categories of processing carried out on behalf of each controller.

The ROPA is often described as the backbone of GDPR compliance because it provides the factual foundation for almost every other obligation. Lawful basis assessments, DPIAs, data subject access requests, breach notifications, and transfer impact assessments all depend on accurate, current processing records. Organizations that treat the ROPA as a one-time documentation exercise rather than a living register quickly find their compliance posture deteriorating as new processing activities are introduced without being recorded.

Supervisory authorities can request the ROPA at any time, and it is typically the first document requested during an investigation or audit. A well-maintained ROPA demonstrates accountability under Art. 5(2) and provides immediate visibility into the organization's data processing landscape. Organizations with more than 250 employees are always required to maintain a ROPA; smaller organizations are exempt only if their processing is occasional, does not include special categories, and is unlikely to result in risk — conditions that few organizations actually meet.
