Article 5(2) introduces the accountability principle: the controller is responsible for, and must be able to demonstrate compliance with, all data protection principles. Article 24 operationalizes this by requiring controllers to implement appropriate technical and organizational measures and to be able to demonstrate that processing is performed in accordance with the GDPR. This goes beyond merely complying — organizations must maintain evidence that they comply, creating an affirmative burden to document decisions, processes, and outcomes.

A robust governance framework starts with comprehensive data protection policies that translate GDPR requirements into operational procedures tailored to the organization's specific processing activities. These policies must be more than aspirational statements — they need to define roles and responsibilities, escalation paths, decision criteria, and review schedules. Policies should be version-controlled, approved by senior management, and communicated to all staff who handle personal data. Regular training ensures that policies are understood and followed, with role-specific training for functions with heightened data protection responsibilities such as marketing, HR, IT, and customer service.

Internal audits provide the feedback loop that turns accountability from a static posture into continuous improvement. An audit program should cover all GDPR domains — from lawful basis and DSR handling to processor management and international transfers — with findings tracked through to remediation. Organizations should maintain a compliance dashboard that provides senior management with visibility into the state of data protection across the organization. When a supervisory authority arrives for an investigation or audit, the organization's ability to produce current, accurate compliance evidence — policies, training records, audit reports, ROPA, DPIA documents, breach register, and processor agreements — is the practical test of whether accountability has been achieved.
