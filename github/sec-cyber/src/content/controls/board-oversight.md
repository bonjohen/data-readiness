Item 106(c) of Regulation S-K requires disclosure of the board's role in overseeing cybersecurity risk. This means identifying which board committee (audit, risk, or a dedicated cyber committee) has oversight responsibility, describing the frequency and nature of briefings the board receives, and explaining the process by which the board is informed about material cybersecurity incidents. The SEC wants investors to understand whether the board is substantively engaged or merely receiving perfunctory updates.

Board cybersecurity governance goes beyond meeting frequency. The quality and depth of briefings matter — boards should receive risk-rated dashboards, incident summaries, third-party assessment results, and updates on remediation progress, not just compliance checklists. The committee charter should explicitly assign cybersecurity oversight responsibility, and minutes should document substantive discussion, questions from directors, and decisions made. When an incident occurs, there should be a defined escalation path from the CISO to the board liaison (typically the committee chair) with expected timelines.

Director expertise is a newer disclosure focus. While the SEC stopped short of requiring a "cyber expert" on every board, companies must describe what cybersecurity-relevant expertise exists among their directors — whether through professional background, certifications, or prior governance experience. Organizations should maintain records of director qualifications and consider whether gaps in board-level cyber expertise represent a governance risk worth addressing through recruitment or education programs.
