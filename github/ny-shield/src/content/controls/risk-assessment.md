Risk assessment is the analytical engine that drives the NY SHIELD Act's reasonable security program requirement. Section 899-bb(2)(b) frames the entire safeguard framework around the identification of "reasonably foreseeable internal and external risks" — language that establishes risk assessment not as an optional best practice but as a statutory prerequisite for all three safeguard categories (administrative, technical, and physical). Without a documented risk assessment, an organization cannot demonstrate that its safeguards are calibrated to the actual threat landscape it faces, which is the core of the reasonableness standard.

The methodology should address both internal risks — employee negligence, insider threats, inadequate training, process failures, system misconfigurations — and external risks including cyberattacks, phishing campaigns, ransomware, third-party breaches, and physical intrusion. For each identified risk, the assessment should evaluate the likelihood of occurrence and the potential impact on the confidentiality, integrity, and availability of private information. The resulting risk ratings inform which safeguards deserve priority investment and which existing controls may be insufficient.

Gap analysis and remediation prioritization close the loop between risk identification and safeguard implementation. Once the assessment reveals where existing controls fall short of addressing identified risks, the organization must develop a remediation plan that prioritizes high-risk gaps while establishing timelines and accountability for lower-risk items. This plan should be treated as a living document, updated as risks evolve, new threats emerge, business processes change, and remediation efforts are completed. The assessment cadence should be at least annual, with interim assessments triggered by significant changes such as new systems, major business shifts, or material security incidents.
