Physical safeguards under § 899-bb(2)(b)(iii) address the tangible protections surrounding private information — the locks, access controls, surveillance systems, and handling procedures that prevent unauthorized physical access. The statute begins with a risk assessment mandate: organizations must evaluate the risks associated with how and where private information is stored, from server rooms and filing cabinets to employee workstations and portable media. This assessment must also cover disposal methods, recognizing that information is vulnerable throughout its lifecycle, including at the point of destruction.

The intrusion detection and response requirement extends security beyond digital boundaries. Organizations must implement physical security measures — access control systems, surveillance cameras, visitor management procedures, and alarm systems — that detect unauthorized attempts to access facilities or areas where private information is maintained. Response procedures must address how the organization reacts to physical security incidents, including lockdown protocols, law enforcement notification, and post-incident assessment of whether private information may have been compromised.

Protection during collection, transportation, and destruction rounds out the physical safeguard requirements. This covers the entire physical journey of private information: secure intake procedures when information is first collected, protected transport methods when moving records between locations or to disposal facilities, and verified destruction at end of life. The statute's explicit mention of "during or after" these activities recognizes that transitional moments — when information is being moved, handed off, or destroyed — are often the most vulnerable points in the physical security chain.
