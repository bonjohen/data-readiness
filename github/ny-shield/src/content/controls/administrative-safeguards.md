The NY SHIELD Act's administrative safeguards form the organizational foundation of a reasonable data security program. Section 899-bb(2)(b)(i) requires businesses to establish management-level accountability by designating one or more employees to coordinate the security program. This coordinator role is responsible for ensuring that the organization systematically identifies internal threats — such as employee negligence, insider misuse, or inadequate access controls — and external threats including cyberattacks, social engineering, and third-party data exposure. The statute does not prescribe specific risk assessment methodologies, but expects organizations to adopt processes proportionate to the size and complexity of their operations.

Employee training is a cornerstone of the administrative safeguards. The statute specifically calls out training and managing employees in security practices and procedures, recognizing that human behavior is the most common vector for data breaches. Training programs should cover the organization's security policies, procedures for handling private information, recognition of phishing and social engineering attacks, and incident reporting protocols. Training must be ongoing rather than a one-time onboarding event, reflecting the evolving threat landscape.

The vendor management requirement under § 899-bb(2)(b)(i)(E) extends the organization's security obligations to its service provider ecosystem. Businesses must select service providers that can demonstrate capability to maintain appropriate safeguards for private information and must require those safeguards contractually. This means due diligence during vendor selection, contractual provisions that specify security requirements, and ongoing monitoring of vendor compliance — creating a chain of accountability that follows private information wherever it flows.
