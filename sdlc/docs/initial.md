1. **CCPA / CPRA privacy readiness** — Help companies map personal data, update privacy notices, define consumer request workflows, document retention, and prepare evidence for California privacy obligations. California describes CCPA as giving consumers control over personal information businesses collect about them. ([California Attorney General][1])

2. **GDPR operational readiness** — Help U.S. SaaS companies selling into Europe identify controller/processor roles, map data flows, document lawful processing bases, and prepare DPIA-style evidence. The European Commission defines controllers as entities deciding why and how personal data is processed. ([European Commission][2])

3. **COPPA readiness** — Help children-facing websites, apps, games, and edtech products review data collection, parental consent flows, privacy notices, and vendor handling. COPPA applies to operators of online services directed to children under 13 or with actual knowledge they collect data from children under 13. ([Federal Trade Commission][3])

4. **FTC Safeguards Rule readiness** — Help fintech, lending, tax, accounting, and similar non-bank financial-service businesses build information security programs, risk assessments, vendor controls, and incident evidence. The FTC says covered financial institutions must develop and maintain an information security program with administrative, technical, and physical safeguards. ([Federal Trade Commission][4])

5. **HIPAA Security Rule readiness** — Help health-tech vendors and business associates document ePHI safeguards, access controls, audit logging, backup practices, incident response, and risk analysis. HHS says the Security Rule requires reasonable and appropriate administrative, physical, and technical safeguards for ePHI. ([HHS.gov][5])

6. **PCI DSS v4.0.1 readiness** — Help companies that touch payment-card data reduce scope, document cardholder-data flows, prepare control evidence, and coordinate with QSAs. PCI DSS v4.0.1 did not change the March 31, 2025 effective date for new requirements. ([PCI Perspectives][6])

7. **NY SHIELD Act data-security readiness** — Help companies holding New York resident data create reasonable security programs, vendor controls, breach-response procedures, and evidence packages. New York’s AG describes the SHIELD Act as strengthening New York data-security and breach-notification law. ([New York State Attorney General][7])

8. **State privacy law patchwork support** — Help companies build reusable privacy operations for Virginia, Colorado, Texas, and similar state laws: rights intake, data inventories, opt-out handling, privacy notices, and processor contracts. Virginia requires clear privacy notices, Colorado requires universal opt-out support, and Texas’s law took effect July 1, 2024. ([Virginia Law][8])

9. **SEC cybersecurity disclosure readiness** — Help public companies or pre-IPO companies document cyber-risk governance, board reporting, incident materiality workflows, and evidence trails. SEC rules require disclosures about cybersecurity risk-management processes, management’s role, and board oversight. ([SEC][9])

10. **AI governance / AI regulatory readiness** — Help AI companies build model inventories, risk classifications, evaluation records, human-review controls, data provenance records, and NIST AI RMF mappings. NIST’s AI RMF is designed to help organizations manage risks to individuals, organizations, and society from AI. ([NIST][10])

11. **EU DORA readiness** — Help fintech, crypto, payments, cloud, SaaS, and ICT vendors that serve EU financial firms document operational resilience, ICT risk management, incident reporting, resilience testing, vendor controls, and third-party risk evidence. DORA entered application on **January 17, 2025** and covers financial entities plus ICT third-party risk management. ([eiopa.europa.eu][11])

12. **SOC 2 readiness** — Help SaaS, AI, data platform, developer tooling, and B2B software companies prepare for SOC 2 Type 1 or Type 2 by documenting systems, mapping controls to the Trust Services Criteria, designing evidence workflows, improving access/change/vendor/security processes, and coordinating audit preparation without claiming to issue the SOC 2 report. AICPA describes SOC as a suite of CPA service offerings connected to system-level controls, and its Trust Services Criteria cover security, availability, processing integrity, confidentiality, and privacy. ([aicpa-cima.com][12]) (already implemented as the soc2 project, but we want to include it here and retire that site)
